Problem 1

In class, we showed how to construct a sequence of unit lower-triangular matrices \(\{L_{k}\}_{k=1,2,\ldots,n-1}\) of the form

\[ L_{k}=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & & \\ & \ddots & & & & \\ & & 1 & & & \\ & & -\ell_{k+1,k} & 1 & & \\ & & \ddots & & \ddots & \\ & & -\ell_{n,k} & & & 1 \end{pmatrix}~, \hbox{ where } \ell_{j,k}=\frac{A_{j,k}}{A_{k,k}},~\hbox{ for } k\leq j \leq n, \]

such that

\[ L_{n-1} L_{n-2} \ldots L_{2} L_{1} A = U, \]

with \(U\) being an upper-triangular matrix. We now want to show in two steps that

\[L:= L_{1}^{-1} L_{2}^{-1} \ldots L_{n-2}^{-1} L_{n-1}^{-1} \]

is a unit lower-triangular matrix.

  1. Show that

\[ L_{k}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & & \\ & \ddots & & & & \\ & & 1 & & & \\ & & \ell_{k+1,k} & 1 & & \\ & & \ddots & & \ddots & \\ & & \ell_{n,k} & & & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Hint: Start by showing that \(L_k=I-\ell_k e_k^{T}\), where \(e_k\) is an \(n \times 1\) vector with a 1 in the \(k^{th}\) entry, and zeros elsewhere, and

\[ \ell_k = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \\ \ell_{k+1,k} \\ \vdots \\ \ell_{n,k} \end{pmatrix}. \]

  1. Now show that

\[L:= L_{1}^{-1} L_{2}^{-1} \ldots L_{n-2}^{-1} L_{n-1}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & \\ \ell_{21} & 1 & & & \\ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 & & \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \\ \ell_{n1} & \ell_{n2} & \cdots & \ell_{n,n-1} & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \]

Solution:

  1. Note that \(e_k^T \ell_k=0\). Therefore, we have:

\[(I+\ell_k e_k^T)L_k = (I+\ell_k e_k^T)(I-\ell_k e_k^T)=I-\ell_k e_k^T \ell_k e_k^T = I,\]

and we conclude that

\[ L_k^{-1}=I+\ell_k e_k^T=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & & \\ & \ddots & & & & \\ & & 1 & & & \\ & & \ell_{k+1,k} & 1 & & \\ & & \ddots & & \ddots & \\ & & \ell_{n,k} & & & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

  1. Using the result of (a), we have

\[ L_{k}^{-1} L_{k+1}^{-1} = (I+\ell_k e_k^T)(I+\ell_{k+1} e_{k+1}^T) = I + \ell_k e_k^T + \ell_{k+1} e_{k+1}^T + \ell_k e_k^T \ell_{k+1} e_{k+1}^T=I + \ell_k e_k^T + \ell_{k+1} e_{k+1}^T,\]

as \(e_k^T \ell_{k+1}=0\). In fact, \(e_k^T \ell_{j}=0\) for \(j \geq k\), and so we can repeat the process until we reach

\[L:= L_{1}^{-1} L_{2}^{-1} \ldots L_{n-2}^{-1} L_{n-1}^{-1} = I + \ell_1 e_1^T + \ell_{2} e_{2}^T + \ldots + \ell_{n-1} e_{n-1}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & \\ \ell_{21} & 1 & & & \\ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 & & \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \\ \ell_{n1} & \ell_{n2} & \cdots & \ell_{n,n-1} & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix}. \]

myLU = function(A,tol=10^-8) {
  n = nrow(A)
  L = diag(x=1,nrow=n)  # start with L = identity matrix
  U=A
  for ( k in 1:(n-1) ) {
    pivot = U[k,k]
    if (abs(pivot) < tol) stop('zero pivot encountered')
    for ( j in (k+1):n ) {
      mult =  U[j,k]/pivot
      U[j,] = U[j,] - mult * U[k,]
      L[j,k] = mult   #  store the multiplier in L
    }
  }
  return(list(L=L,U=U))
}

Problem 3

This problem is taken from the in-class portion of an old midterm.

You are trying to solve a linear system of four equations:

\[\left[\begin{array}{ccc} && \\ & A & \\ && \\ && \end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{c}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ -2 \\ 4 \\ 2.25 \end{array}\right]~,\] but unfortunately \(A\) is a black box and you cannot see the contents. Oh my! Luckily, from the physics of the problem, you do have three pieces of information about the system and the solution:

  • \(||x||_{\infty}=2\)

  • The condition number of \(A\), using the \(\infty\)-norm, is 32

  • \(\left[\begin{array}{ccc} && \\ & A & \\ && \\ && \end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{r}2.0750 \\ 0.1625 \\ 0.4500 \\ -0.1875 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ -2 \\ 4 \\ 2.1875 \end{array}\right]\)

Is this information enough to determine the solution \(x\) to \(Ax=b\)? If yes, find \(x\). If no, say as much as you can about \(x\).

Solution:

We have \[\frac{\hbox{relative forward error}}{\hbox{relative backward error}}=\frac{\frac{||x-x_a||_{\infty}}{||x||_{\infty}}}{\frac{||Ax_a-b||_{\infty}}{||b||_{\infty}}} =\frac{\frac{||x-x_a||_{\infty}}{2}}{\frac{2.25-2.1875}{4}} \leq 32 = \hbox{ condition number of }A, \] so \(||x-x_a||_{\infty} \leq 1\), where \(x_a=\left[\begin{array}{r}2.0750 \\ 0.1625 \\ 0.4500 \\ -0.1875 \end{array}\right]\). Combined with the fact that one of the components of \(x\) must have an absolute value of 2, we can conclude that \(x_1=2\). For the other components, we can only say that they are within 1 of the respective components of \(x_a\). So, in summary, we have

\[x_1=2\] \[-0.8375 \leq x_2 \leq 1.1625\] \[-0.55 \leq x_3 \leq 1.45 \] \[-1.1875 \leq x_4 \leq 0.8125 \]

Problem 4

A real \(n \times n\) matrix \(Q\) is orthonormal if

\[ Q^{T}Q=QQ^T=I;~i.e., Q^{-1}=Q^T.\]

Note: sometimes you will see these matrices just called orthogonal matrices. The analagous matrices that contain complex entries (\(Q^*Q=QQ^*=I\), where the \(~^*\) is a conjugate transpose) are called unitary.

Show that \(||Q||_2=||Q^{-1}||_2=1\), and therefore the 2-norm condition number of any orthonormal matrix \(Q\) is \(\kappa_2(Q)=||Q||_2 ||Q^{-1}||_2=1\).

Solution:

For all \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\), we have

\[||Qx||_2^2 = x^T Q^T Q x = x^T x = ||x||_2^2,\]

and therefore \(||Qx||_2=||x||_2\). So

\[||Q||_2 = \max_{x~s.t. ||x||_2 \neq 0} \frac{||Qx||_2}{||x||_2} = 1.\]

The same line of reasoning applies to \(Q^{-1}\), since for all \(x\), we have

\[||Q^{-1}x||_2^2 = x^T (Q^{-1})^T Q^{-1} x = x^T Q Q^{T} x = x^T x = ||x||_2^2. \] Therefore, \(\kappa_2(Q)=||Q||_2 ||Q^{-1}||_2=1\).

Cond = function(A,p=2) {
  if (p == 2) {  # by default use the 
    s = svd(A)$d
    s = s[s>0]
    return(max(s)/min(s))
  }
  if (p == 1) {  # use the 1 norm
    Ainv = solve(A)
    return(max(colSums(abs(A)))*max(colSums(abs(Ainv))))
  }
  if (p == 'I') {   # use the infinity norm
    Ainv = solve(A)
    return(max(rowSums(abs(A)))*max(rowSums(abs(Ainv))))
  }
}

Problem 6

This was Question 5 on the activity on norms.

Prove that \[ ||A||_{\infty}=\max_{1 \leq i \leq n}\left\{ \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| \right\}=\text{maximum absolute row sum}.\]

Solution:

We need to show LHS=\(||A||_{\infty} \leq \max_{1 \leq i \leq n}\left\{ \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| \right\}\)=RHS and RHS \(\leq\) LHS.

Step 1: Show LHS \(\leq\) RHS

\[\begin{align*} ||A||_{\infty} &= \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1}||Ax||_{\infty} \text{ (by Question 4 on the activity on norms)} \\ &= \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} \left\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |a_i^T x | \right\} \text{ (where } a_i^T \text{ is the i-th row of matrix A)}\\ &= \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} \left\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \left| \left\{ \sum_{j=1}^n A_{ij} x_j \right\} \right| \right\} \\ &\leq \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} \left\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij} x_j| \right\} \text{(since taking the absolute value first and then summing is larger than taking the absolute value after summing)} \\ &= \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} \left\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| | x_j| \right\}\\ &\leq \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} \left\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| \right\} \text{ (since} ||x||_{\infty} \leq 1 \implies |x_j|\leq 1 \text{ for all } j) \end{align*}\]

Step 2: Show RHS \(\leq\) LHS

Let \(k= \underset{1 \leq i \leq n}{\operatorname{argmax}} \left\{ \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| \right\}\). Then \(\sum_{j=1}^n |A_{kj}| = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |A_{ij}|=\text{maximum row sum.}\) Now, let \(y\) be a vector with \(y_j = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{ if } A_{kj}\geq 0 \\ -1 & \text{ if } A_{kj} < 0\end{cases}\).

Then,

\[\begin{align*} \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |A_{ij}| &=\sum_{j=1}^n |A_{kj}| \\ &=|\sum_{j=1}^n A_{kj} y_j| \text{ (notice signs cancel)} \\ &=a_k^T y \\ &\leq ||A y ||_{\infty} \text{ (since norm is max entry in } Ay \text{ and } a_k^Ty \text{ is just one entry in vector ) Ay} \\ &\leq \max_{||x||_{\infty}=1} ||A x ||_{\infty} \text{ (since } y \subseteq \{x \text{ such that } ||x||_{\infty}=1\} \text{ see Trick 2 on the activity on norms )} \\ &= ||A || _{\infty} \end{align*}\]

Therefore, LHS \(\leq\) RHS and RHS \(\leq\) LHS, so \[||A||_{\infty}=\max_{1 \leq i \leq n}\left\{ \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}| \right\}=\text{maximum absolute row sum}.\]

Problem 7

Solve the following system by finding the \(PA=LU\) factorization and then carrying out the two-step back substitution (all by hand):

\[ A=\begin{pmatrix}-1 & 0 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2\\ x_3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}.\]

Solution:

First, let \(U=A\) and we will keep a matrix \(P=I\) for all of the permutations. Let’s exchange the first two rows, so that 3 is the pivot: \[U = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad P = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\] Next we eliminate the first column and store the multipliers in \(L=I\): \[ U= \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{4}{3} \\ 0 & -\frac{2}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \end{pmatrix} \qquad L= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{3} & 1 & 0 \\ \frac{2}{3} & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad P = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\] Now we exchange the second and third rows to get the \(-2/3\) in \(U\) to be the pivot, making sure to also exchange the below diagonal elements in the corresponding rows in \(L\): \[ U= \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -\frac{2}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{4}{3} \end{pmatrix} \qquad L= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{2}{3} & 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{3} & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad P = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \] Now we eliminate the second column: \[U=\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -\frac{2}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ 0 & 0 & 1.5 \end{pmatrix} \qquad L= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{2}{3} & 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{3} & -\frac{1}{2} & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad P = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
